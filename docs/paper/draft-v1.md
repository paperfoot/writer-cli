## Abstract

Large language models generate fluent text from a collapsed stylistic distribution — the same em-dashes, the same transitional phrases, the same structural patterns regardless of prompt. Post-hoc "humanisation" merely adds a second layer of uniformity. We present *writer*, an open-source system that reverses this collapse by capturing an author's stylometric fingerprint from their corpus and using it to both fine-tune and constrain generation. Our approach combines three mechanisms: (1) a multi-dimensional stylometric fingerprint spanning lexical, syntactic, punctuation, readability, and vocabulary richness features, computed from as few as 400 writing samples; (2) LoRA fine-tuning on a quantised 26B-parameter model (Gemma 4) using the author's corpus in chat format; and (3) a quality decoding pipeline that generates multiple candidates and ranks them by stylometric distance from the target fingerprint. On a case study of Douglas Adams' complete works (684K words, 9 books), the system reduces stylometric distance from 0.306 (base model) to 0.241 (fine-tuned + ranked), with the target ceiling at 0.121 (held-out human text). We identify the specific stylometric dimensions where current approaches fall short — syntactic n-gram patterns, sentence-initial distributions, and discourse marker profiles — and propose extensions grounded in the authorship attribution literature. We argue that the richness of human language was about *difference*, not correctness, and that recovering individual voice is a more faithful goal than optimising for generic fluency.

## 1. Introduction

The proliferation of large language models has produced an irony: tools designed to generate human language have made human language more uniform. A passage generated by GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, or Llama is more stylistically similar to passages from the other three than any of them are to the author whose work they were trained on. This is not a bug in any individual model — it is a structural property of training on internet-scale corpora and optimising for RLHF-derived preferences that reward clarity, safety, and a particular register of educated informality.

The consequence is a collapse of linguistic diversity. Douglas Adams does not write like Ernest Hemingway. Hemingway does not write like Virginia Woolf. The differences between them — in sentence rhythm, vocabulary choice, syntactic structure, punctuation habits, paragraph cadence — are not noise to be smoothed away. They are the signal. They are what makes each voice recognisable, what makes reading one author a different experience from reading another.

Current approaches to personalised generation fall into three categories, each with fundamental limitations:

1. **Prompt engineering** — instructing the model to "write like Adams" produces text that matches a caricature of the author's most obvious traits (for Adams: jokes about space, mentions of towels) while missing the structural fingerprint that actually defines the voice. Wang et al. (2025) demonstrated this systematically: across 40,000 generations and 400 authors, LLMs achieved only 19–65% authorship verification accuracy for informal writing styles, even with few-shot in-context examples.

2. **Post-hoc humanisation** — tools that paraphrase LLM output to evade AI detection do not restore individual voice. They replace one uniform distribution with another. The output reads as "not obviously AI" rather than "distinctly human."

3. **Fine-tuning without stylometric grounding** — LoRA and full fine-tuning on an author's corpus shifts the model's distribution toward the training data, but without a principled measurement of *which* dimensions of style matter, the model may learn content patterns (Adams writes about space) rather than structural patterns (Adams averages 6.8 words per sentence with a standard deviation of 4.3).

We propose a fourth approach: **stylometrically-grounded generation**, where the target voice is first decomposed into a measurable fingerprint, then used to both train and evaluate the model. The system does not ask "does this sound vaguely like Adams?" — it asks "does this text exhibit sentence length distribution X, function word frequencies Y, punctuation patterns Z, and vocabulary richness W, within tolerance of the target fingerprint?"

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Computational Stylometry

The field of authorship attribution provides the theoretical foundation for measuring writing style. The key insight, established over decades of PAN Shared Tasks (2011–2025) and formalised in surveys by Stamatatos (2009), Lagutina et al. (2019), and Habib et al. (2025), is that reliable authorship features are those that are *content-independent*: they capture how an author writes, not what they write about.

The feature categories, ranked by discriminative power across the literature, are:

1. **Character n-grams** (2–4 characters) — consistently the single strongest feature category (Sapkota et al., NAACL 2015). They implicitly capture morphology, punctuation habits, and common word fragments.
2. **Function word frequencies** — closed-class words (the, of, and, but, however) whose frequency distributions are highly author-specific and topic-independent (Mosteller & Wallace, 1963; Argamon & Levitan, 2005).
3. **POS tag n-grams** — syntactic patterns that capture how an author constructs sentences at a grammatical level (Sidorov et al., 2013; Posadas-Durán et al., 2014). POS trigrams built from dependency parse paths outperform surface n-grams.
4. **Syntactic dependency patterns** — deeper structural features from parse trees (Eder & Rybicki, DHQ 2022), achieving >90% attribution accuracy for segments >300 tokens.
5. **Punctuation profiles** — em-dash frequency, semicolon usage, question and exclamation density (Baayen et al., 1996; Grieve, 2007).
6. **Sentence and word length distributions** — simple but surprisingly effective features first used by Mendenhall (1887) and consistently included in modern systems.
7. **Vocabulary richness** — type-token ratio, Yule's K (1944), Simpson's D (1949), hapax legomena ratio.

### 2.2 Neural Authorship Representations

Rivera-Soto et al. (EMNLP 2021) introduced LUAR, a transformer-based model that learns authorship embeddings from sets of documents. Wang et al. (TACL 2023) probed these embeddings and confirmed they encode genuine stylistic signal, not merely topic. Alshomary et al. (EMNLP 2025) extended this with *Layered Insights*, showing that utilising all transformer layers via contrastive loss improves out-of-domain authorship attribution by ~50% over standard LUAR — lower layers capture lexical/surface features while higher layers capture discourse/semantic patterns.

The emerging consensus is that hybrid systems — hand-crafted stylometric features combined with neural embeddings — outperform either approach alone (Alshomary et al., 2025).

### 2.3 Personalised Generation

The problem of generating text in a specific author's voice has received increasing attention. Fine-tuning approaches (LoRA, QLoRA) shift the model's output distribution toward the training corpus but do not explicitly optimise for stylometric fidelity. Zhang et al. (ACL 2025) introduced *StyleVector*, a training-free method that represents personal style as a vector in the LLM's activation space, achieving 8% improvement over baselines with 1,700× less storage than parameter-efficient methods. Ionio Research (2026) replicated this for literary authors, finding that mid-level transformer layers (layer 5 from the end) encode the richest stylistic abstractions.

Contrastive decoding (Li et al., 2023; CoPe, EMNLP 2025) offers another axis: subtracting the base model's logits from the fine-tuned model's logits amplifies what the fine-tuning learned, suppressing the generic distribution that both models share.

## 3. System Architecture

### 3.1 Overview

*writer* is a command-line tool implemented in Rust with a Python bridge for MLX inference on Apple Silicon. The pipeline has four stages:

1. **Corpus ingestion** — the author's works are cleaned, normalised (preserving stylometrically-relevant whitespace and punctuation), deduplicated, and chunked into samples of 500–2,000 words.
2. **Fingerprint computation** — a `StylometricFingerprint` is computed from the corpus, capturing 9 feature categories across ~50 individual metrics.
3. **LoRA fine-tuning** — a rank-16 LoRA adapter is trained on the corpus in chat format using `mlx_lm.lora`, teaching the model the author's content patterns, vocabulary, and natural completions.
4. **Quality decoding** — at generation time, N candidates are produced, each scored against the fingerprint, and the best is returned. Candidates that fail structural filters (excessive AI-slop density, extreme deviation from target distributions) are rejected and regenerated.

### 3.2 Stylometric Fingerprint

The fingerprint comprises:

| Category | Metrics | Computation |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Sentence length | mean, SD, median, p95 | Unicode sentence segmentation |
| Word length | mean, SD, median, p95 | Token-level character count |
| Paragraph length | mean, SD, median, p95 | Newline-delimited blocks |
| Function words | frequency distribution over ~300 words | Normalised per-word counts |
| N-gram profile | character bigrams and trigrams | Top-K frequency vectors |
| Punctuation | em-dashes, questions, exclamations, semicolons, colons, parentheses per 1K words | Pattern matching |
| Readability | Flesch-Kincaid grade, Coleman-Liau, ARI, avg syllables/word | Standard formulae |
| Vocabulary richness | TTR, Yule's K, hapax legomena ratio, hapax dislegomena ratio, Simpson's D | Frequency spectrum analysis |
| AI-slop detection | banned word/phrase density | Curated list of LLM-characteristic terms |

The distance between a generated text and the target fingerprint is computed as a weighted combination of normalised differences across all dimensions, with weights derived from the authorship attribution literature's feature importance rankings.

### 3.3 Quality Decoding Pipeline

The decoding pipeline wraps every generation call:

```
Input prompt
    → System prompt priming (soft stylometric guidance)
    → Logit bias (boost preferred vocabulary, suppress banned words)
    → Generate N candidates
    → Score each against fingerprint (stylometric distance)
    → Rank by distance
    → Filter top candidate (reject if AI-slop density > threshold)
    → Return best, or regenerate
```

This pipeline ensures that no generated text is returned without being measured against the target voice. Quality is opt-out, not opt-in.

### 3.4 Adapter Training

LoRA adapters are trained using `mlx_lm.lora` on Apple Silicon with the following configuration for large models (26B parameters):

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Rank | 16 | Standard for style adaptation |
| Learning rate | 2×10⁻⁵ | Conservative for large models |
| Batch size | 1 | Memory constraint (26B in 4-bit ≈ 15 GB) |
| Sequence length | 2,048 | Sufficient for most writing samples |
| Steps | 500 | Empirically determined convergence point |

Training data is formatted as chat examples: the user message requests a passage in a specific context tag (longform, dialogue, narrative), and the assistant message contains the actual corpus text. This teaches the model to produce the author's text as its natural completion.

## 4. Case Study: Douglas Adams

### 4.1 Corpus

We ingested the complete published works of Douglas Adams: 9 books, 684,089 words, yielding 492 training samples after chunking and deduplication. The corpus was split 90/10 into 441 training and 49 validation samples.

### 4.2 Fingerprint

Key features of the Adams fingerprint:

| Feature | Adams | Typical LLM output |
|---------|-------|-------------------|
| Sentence length mean | 6.8 words | 15–25 words |
| Sentence length SD | 4.3 | 8–12 |
| Word length mean | 4.44 chars | 5.0–5.5 chars |
| Flesch-Kincaid grade | 3.9 | 10–14 |
| Questions per 1K words | 8.95 | 0–2 |
| Exclamations per 1K words | 5.24 | 0–1 |
| Yule's K | 85.3 | 40–60 |
| Hapax legomena ratio | 24.7% | 15–20% |

Adams writes in extremely short sentences (mean 6.8 words, roughly half the length of typical LLM output), uses frequent rhetorical questions and exclamations, maintains a 4th-grade reading level despite sophisticated ideas, and has an unusually rich vocabulary (high Yule's K, high hapax ratio).

### 4.3 Results

| Condition | Stylometric Distance | Notes |
|-----------|---------------------|-------|
| Held-out Adams text | 0.121 | Ceiling — real Adams scored against Adams fingerprint |
| Gemma 4 26B + LoRA + ranked | 0.241 | Best generated, with adapter and candidate ranking |
| Gemma 4 26B + LoRA (unranked) | 0.290 | With adapter, no candidate ranking |
| Gemma 4 26B baseline | 0.306 | No adapter, Ollama inference |
| Gemma 3 4B + LoRA (200 steps) | — | Proof-of-concept, qualitatively repetitive |

The LoRA adapter reduced distance by 5% (0.306 → 0.290) and candidate ranking added another 17% improvement (0.290 → 0.241). The combined system closes 35% of the gap between the base model and the held-out human text ceiling.

### 4.4 Qualitative Observations

The fine-tuned model produces text with Adams-like properties:
- Short declarative sentences alternating with longer ones
- Dialogue in Adams' characteristic rhythm (short exchanges, deadpan observations)
- Adams-universe vocabulary (characters, places, concepts)
- Absurdist humour emerging from matter-of-fact descriptions of impossible situations

However, specific stylometric dimensions remain uncaptured:
- **Rhetorical questions** — Adams averages 8.95 per 1K words; generated text produces 0
- **Exclamations** — 5.24 per 1K words in Adams; 0 in generated text
- **Sentence-initial patterns** — Adams heavily uses conjunctions as sentence starters ("And", "But", "So"); the model produces standard subject-verb openings

## 5. Gap Analysis and Proposed Extensions

Based on our results and the authorship attribution literature, we identify the following gaps and propose targeted extensions:

### 5.1 POS Tag N-grams

Our fingerprint captures lexical and surface-level features but misses syntactic structure. POS tag trigrams are the third most discriminative feature category in the literature (Sidorov et al., 2013) and would capture Adams' distinctive grammatical patterns — sentence fragments, coordinate clause chains, and non-standard sentence openings.

### 5.2 Sentence-Initial Distributions

Feng et al. (EMNLP 2012) showed that sentence-opening POS patterns are content-independent and strongly discriminative. Adams' heavy use of conjunction-initial sentences ("And then nothing happened." "But it didn't.") is a signature feature that our current system fails to capture or reward.

### 5.3 Discourse Marker Profiles

Adams uses specific discourse markers at characteristic frequencies — "quite", "rather", "actually", "simply", "in fact" — that distinguish his voice from other British authors. A targeted discourse marker frequency profile (tracking ~50 markers per 1K words) would capture this dimension.

### 5.4 Activation Steering

StyleVector (Zhang et al., ACL 2025) and contrastive activation vectors (Ionio, 2026) offer a complementary generation mechanism: representing the target voice as a vector in the model's activation space and adding it during forward passes. This could be combined with our existing LoRA adapter — the LoRA shifts the weight distribution while the style vector steers individual forward passes. The Ionio results showed strongest effects at mid-level transformer layers.

### 5.5 Contrastive Decoding

CoPe (EMNLP 2025) subtracts the base model's logit distribution from the fine-tuned model's distribution, amplifying the stylistic shift that LoRA learned. This is particularly promising for our use case: the base Gemma 4 model has a strong "generic LLM voice" that partially overwrites the LoRA's training. Contrastive decoding would suppress exactly that generic component.

### 5.6 Stylometric DPO: Turning Fingerprint Dimensions into Training Signal

The most significant architectural improvement would be to use the stylometric fingerprint not merely as a post-hoc scoring function, but as a training signal via Direct Preference Optimisation (Rafailov et al., 2023). The procedure is:

1. **Generate candidate pairs** — for each training prompt, generate two completions from the SFT-tuned model with different temperatures or seeds.
2. **Score both against the fingerprint** — compute stylometric distance for each.
3. **Construct DPO pairs** — the completion with lower stylometric distance becomes the *chosen* response; the one with higher distance becomes *rejected*.
4. **Train DPO adapter** — fine-tune on top of the SFT LoRA, teaching the model to prefer outputs that match the target fingerprint across all measured dimensions.

This approach is more powerful than post-hoc filtering because it modifies the model's distribution directly. Where candidate ranking can only select from what the model produces, DPO shifts what the model *wants* to produce. If the fingerprint penalises long sentences, the model learns to prefer short ones — not because it was told "write short sentences" in a prompt, but because the preference data consistently rewards shorter completions when they better match the target.

A further extension is **Stylometric RLHF** using a reward model trained on fingerprint features. The reward model would take a text and output a scalar score based on stylometric distance. This could be used with PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) or GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) for online optimisation, though the computational cost is substantially higher than DPO.

## 6. Toward a Full-Stack Voice Architecture

Based on the gap analysis, we propose a complete pipeline that integrates stylometric intelligence at every stage of the generation process:

```
Corpus
  |
  v
Fingerprint (expanded: lexical + syntactic + POS n-grams + discourse markers
              + sentence-initial distributions + LUAR embeddings)
  |
  +---> SFT LoRA (learns content + surface style from corpus)
  |       |
  |       v
  |     DPO with stylometric pairs (learns WHICH style properties to optimise)
  |       |
  |       v
  +---> Generation with:
  |       - Activation steering (StyleVector at layer -5)
  |       - Contrastive decoding (subtract base model logits)
  |       - Logit bias (vocabulary preferences from fingerprint)
  |
  v
Candidate ranking (fingerprint distance + LUAR embedding similarity)
  |
  v
Output
```

Each layer addresses a different failure mode:

| Layer | Failure it addresses | Mechanism |
|-------|---------------------|-----------|
| SFT LoRA | Model doesn't know the author's vocabulary or topics | Supervised fine-tuning on corpus |
| Stylometric DPO | Model produces correct content but wrong rhythm/structure | Preference learning from fingerprint-scored pairs |
| Activation steering | Model's generic voice overwrites fine-tuning | Direct hidden state intervention at stylistically-rich layers |
| Contrastive decoding | Base distribution dominates fine-tuned distribution | Logit subtraction amplifies what LoRA learned |
| Candidate ranking | Individual generations vary in quality | Score and select from multiple samples |

The key insight is that these mechanisms are *composable*. LoRA and DPO modify the model's parameters. Activation steering and contrastive decoding modify inference-time computation. Candidate ranking operates on outputs. No two layers compete; each operates at a different level of abstraction.

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1 Voice is Not Humour

A common misconception is that capturing an author's "voice" means capturing their humour, their topics, or their worldview. These are aspects of *content*. Voice, in the stylometric sense, is *form*: how sentences are constructed, which words are chosen over synonyms, how punctuation is deployed, what rhythm the prose has. Adams' voice is not "jokes about space" — it is 6.8-word sentences, rhetorical questions at 9 per 1K words, 4th-grade readability with graduate-level ideas, and a vocabulary richness (Yule's K = 85.3) that exceeds most literary fiction.

Our system deliberately separates these concerns. The stylometric fingerprint captures form. The LoRA fine-tuning captures both form and content. The quality decoding pipeline filters by form. An ideal system would generate text that a stylometric analyser cannot distinguish from the target author — regardless of whether the topic is space, biology, or a corporate biography.

### 7.2 Linguistic Diversity as a Goal

The motivation for this work is not impersonation. It is the observation that LLMs have inadvertently narrowed the bandwidth of written language. When every email, every blog post, every draft is polished through the same model, the result is a monoculture. Individual quirks — the em-dash habits, the sentence fragment preferences, the vocabulary choices that make each writer recognisable — are smoothed into a median voice.

We argue that recovering and preserving individual voice is a legitimate goal for language technology. A writer who wants their AI assistant to draft in *their* voice, not in the model's voice, needs a system that understands what their voice actually is. Stylometric fingerprinting provides that understanding.

### 7.3 Limitations

- **Corpus size requirements** — our approach requires hundreds of writing samples (we used 441 training samples from 684K words). Authors with small corpora may not produce stable fingerprints.
- **Genre sensitivity** — Adams' fingerprint is computed from novels. His newspaper columns, speeches, or emails would have different stylometric profiles. A production system would need genre-aware fingerprinting.
- **Evaluation** — stylometric distance is a proxy for perceptual voice fidelity. A text with distance 0.20 is not necessarily "twice as good" as one with distance 0.40. Human evaluation remains the ground truth.
- **Factual accuracy** — the fine-tuned model hallucinated facts when generating a biography, adding details not in the prompt. Stylometric fidelity and factual fidelity are orthogonal problems.

---

## 8. Conclusion

We have presented *writer*, an open-source system for voice-faithful text generation that combines stylometric fingerprinting with LoRA fine-tuning and quality-ranked decoding. On a case study of Douglas Adams, the system closes 35% of the gap between generic LLM output and the target voice, with clear paths to improvement through syntactic features, activation steering, and contrastive decoding.

The broader argument is that linguistic diversity — the property that makes Adams sound different from Hemingway, that makes your writing sound different from anyone else's — is worth preserving and recovering. Language models should be tools that amplify individual voice, not tools that replace it with a median.

Code and fingerprint data are available at https://github.com/199-biotechnologies/writer.
